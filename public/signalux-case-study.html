Subtle: Surfacing What Users Don’t Say

My role

Product designer & builder

Timeline

2025-ongoing

Team

Solo, self-led

Platform

Web (responsive), Next.js

Overview

Subtle is a multimodal UX research assistant for moderated user tests and interviews. It adds a second read on timing and behavior next to what people say out loud (cursor rhythm, pauses and repeats, voice and session flow, and a light face layer for stillness and timing), then turns that into interpreted lines, a replayable timeline, and clips for stakeholders who were not in the room.

The problem

The insight I could not shake: the hardest evidence is often behavioral and time-based, yet our default workflow still lets the transcript win by default.

In practice, spoken answers carry synthesis first, while hesitation, idle stretches, and repeated passes through the same UI rarely make it into the deck. The most convincing stretch of a session often surfaced for me after the participant had left, after the team had aligned, and after the transcript had already won the argument. I wanted that layer legible while the session was still open, not only in the rewatch.

What I did

From research framing through working software: multimodal capture, interpreted readouts, and review flows I could stress with real moderated sessions.

  • Built and shipped the prototype in Next.js, including capture paths and timeline replay I could run end to end
  • Designed interaction and UI for user testing and interview modes, dashboard readouts, clip export, and iteration toggles between early and later directions
  • Named scope and limits (what I did not build) so the work stayed defensible as a research instrument, not an analytics platform

Subtle sits beside what people say: I pull hesitation, timing, and light behavioral context from cursor, voice, and session flow, and surface friction that rarely becomes a tidy quote. I am not trying to automate judgment; I am trying to notice in time to ask one more question before the room closes.

Why I kept circling this problem, and the insight that stuck

Why it mattered to me. In my tests and interviews I kept seeing the same split: on the record, fine; off the record, pauses, retraces, and stuttered movement through the flow. I felt the cost immediately, but the evidence thinned the second I tried to write it down, or it lost to cleaner quotes when I synthesized later.

I still build decks from quotes, completion, and obvious errors. I also know how much lives under that: the pause before a confident click, the third pass over the same corner, the answer that lands late. That stack is real load even when language smooths it. I wanted to treat that layer with the same seriousness I give a screenshot people can see.

What locked in

Once I named the pattern, the tension was obvious: I could feel behavioral load in the moment, but the record that traveled was still biased toward what people said and what the transcript highlighted.

What if those signals lived in the session while I could still probe, not only in the rewatch?

That question became the spine of Subtle. It is not a replacement for me as a researcher. It is a deliberate second read on timing and behavior while the session is still warm enough to interrogate, before the story hardens.

What I built: from signals to something I can show

I did not want another methodology deck. I wanted a loop I could run: modalities I trust, interpretations my team can disagree with kindly, then a timeline and clips that survive the handoff. That is the spine of what I shipped in this slice.

  • Capture: I record cursor rhythm, pauses, repeats; I add a minimal face read for timing and stillness, not mood labels.
  • Interpret: I fold cues into hesitation, friction, uncertainty, and load; every flag carries confidence, a short reason, and visible ambiguity.
  • Deliver: I give myself live readouts, a post-session timeline with replay, and exports for people who were not in the room.

I started from plain-language lines: “hesitated before acting,” “repeated interactions suggest friction”, because raw charts alone did not survive a critique. Then I obsessed over how much context I get at each moment (toggle below). Clips became my bridge for stakeholders who never sat in the session.

My first pass: one session summary and a single timeline of ticks. It proved the idea, but it felt thin for how I actually reason: I was still doing too much inference in my head.

I pushed toward per-finding depth: a frame or capture context next to each flag, so I read “why” beside “when” before I commit to a rewatch.

  • Each signal gets a visual anchor, not only a tick on a bar.
  • I can challenge an interpretation without losing the moment it came from.

I still tie every signal to time and interaction context; what I have not settled is how dense that bundle should feel during synthesis versus when I hand it off.

  • I export the moment that persuaded me, not a generic highlight reel.

Every flag still ties to timing and multimodal context; what I am still calibrating is density for live moderation versus what I hand off async.

How I scoped it, and where I refused to pretend

I drew a hard line on emotions. I stuck to observable timing and movement; synthetic mood labels felt brittle and impossible to defend in a critique.

I favored interpretation over raw metrics. I wanted speed and path to become sentences my team could actually argue about.

I treated outputs as hypotheses. I combined modalities, kept the signal set small, and left ambiguity visible so sessions stayed legible.

What I put in, and what I cut

My goal was to prove behavioral signals change how I research, not to ship an analytics platform. I built: cursor tracking, hesitation and friction reads, a light face layer, timeline with replay, dashboard, clips. I deferred: full recording, heavy ML, real backend scale. Naming what I did not build matters: it is where policy, engineering, and ethics work would land next.

Honest limits

  • Signals stay probabilistic; I read them as prompts, not proof.
  • Iframes still cap what I can capture in some setups.
  • The face layer is timing context, not mood detection.
  • I explored this at small scale: enough to learn, not enough to claim universal truth.

What changed for me, and what I would tackle next

This work changed how fast I trust my own memory after a session. I synthesize with fewer “I think something was off there” gaps, I probe live with a little more courage, and I walk into stakeholder conversations with clips tied to the same objects I used privately.

The shift I care about: I am designing for what people struggle to say out loud, not only what prints cleanly on a transcript.

If I pushed this into a real program, I would chase honest recording and replay, voice detail where policy allows, tighter Figma and prototype ingestion, and collaboration primitives, then I would revisit every capture default with a privacy engineer in the room.

Subtle stays a sketch on purpose: humble interpretation, me still in charge. I built it to shrink blind spots, not to crown a model as the researcher.

I am not trying to detect truth; I am trying to notice sooner.

If AI helps here, it should sharpen what I see, not replace the question I ask next.
