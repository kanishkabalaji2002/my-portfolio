Vernacular architecture

A mud house, built by hand.

A study in cellular earth construction: from first sketches and mind-maps to a layered physical model with organic walls, a sloped roof, and the slow logic of mud, straw, and structure.

01

Ideation

The project began as a loose brain-dump: keywords, adjacencies, and what “home” might mean in mud—thermal mass, thick walls, small openings, and rooms that grow from simple cells rather than a rigid grid.

Sketches on paper mapped tensions between enclosure and light, between a single sweeping roof and the irregular footprint of the plan.

02

Cellular organising

Floor plans evolved from discrete circular “cells” that merged into one continuous organic shape—closer to growth in nature than to Cartesian room division. A wireframe study clarified volume before color and texture took over in watercolor-style perspectives of interior and exterior light on mud surfaces.

A labeled final plan anchored the model: one readable footprint the build could follow inch by inch.

03

Construction

Hands in wet clay, walls built up in thin, wavy courses for strength and texture, and the hollow cellular section of the thick mud shell became visible as the mass rose. The process is slow, tactile, and unforgiving—each layer waits on the last.

04

Roof & final assembly

A light wooden armature defines the roof geometry; triangular panels close the form and meet the irregular mud walls at a careful junction—organic base, disciplined cap. Final shots read the model in the round: overhang, window cuts, and the depth of the earth walls.
